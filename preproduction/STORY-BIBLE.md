# Vow of Smoke — Setting Bible
**Lore, cosmology, and world reference for the Vow of Smoke universe**

---

## The Old Gods

The gods are not creators. They did not make the world, and they do not rule it. They are the world's immune system — living expressions of the places that gave birth to them. A spring that ran for ten thousand years eventually dreamed itself a consciousness. A grove where generations buried their dead eventually exhaled something with a voice. The gods are what happens when a place exists long enough to become *aware.*

They are not omnipotent. They cannot leave their sacred sites. They do not answer prayers, grant miracles, or interfere in human affairs. What they do is *sustain.* A god's presence stabilizes the land around it — regulates groundwater, prevents erosion, keeps fault lines quiet, maintains the deep equilibrium that humans take for granted and call geology. They are the reason the ground holds.

### Nature and Form

Gods have no fixed physical form. In the rare instances they manifest visibly, they appear as impressions of their origin — the spring god is flowing water shaped like a person, the grove god is a figure of tangled root and branch, the god of dust is a young face with ancient eyes. These forms are approximations. The gods are not bodies. They are *conditions.*

Each god has a domain defined by its origin:
- **Elemental gods** arise from natural features — springs, groves, stone formations, river confluences
- **Threshold gods** arise from places of transition — crossroads, harbors, mountain passes
- **Memory gods** arise from places of accumulated human presence — libraries, temples, marketplaces that stood for centuries

The oldest gods predate human settlement. The youngest are several hundred years old. No new gods have been born in the modern era — concrete and steel don't dream.

### The Divine Lifecycle

Gods are not immortal. They are sustained by the integrity of their sacred site and by the living memory of the people who once tended them. A god whose temple is demolished doesn't die immediately — the roots go deeper than the foundation. But a god whose site is paved over *and* forgotten begins to fade. This is a process that takes centuries. It is not painful. It is a slow dimming, like a candle burning down.

A god that dies naturally releases its accumulated energy back into the earth. The transition is gentle. The land settles. The groundwater adjusts. Life goes on.

A god that is killed — its energy ripped away by force — leaves a wound. The land above the site destabilizes. Sinkholes, structural failures, water contamination, localized seismic events. The infrastructure built on the bones of the divine collapses because the bones were load-bearing.

### Divine Speech

Gods communicate rarely and with difficulty. Their consciousness operates on geological timescales — a thought that takes a human a second takes a god a season. When they do speak (usually in moments of crisis or death), their voices carry the quality of their origin. The spring god sounds like water over stone. The god of forgotten places sounds like dust settling in an empty room. The god of the city's heart sounds like the city itself — traffic and breath and a million overlapping lives.

## The Meridian

The Meridian is a network. Every sacred site in the city is a node, and the connections between them form a web of divine energy running through the bedrock. The ancients understood this intuitively — they built their temples at the nodes and their roads along the connections. The modern city was built on top of this architecture without knowing it existed.

### Function

The Meridian serves three purposes:

1. **Stabilization.** The divine energy flowing through the network reinforces the geological foundations of the city. It is the reason the city has never experienced a major earthquake despite sitting on an active fault system.

2. **Communication.** The gods are isolated by nature — bound to their sites, unable to travel. The Meridian allows them to sense each other. Not speak, exactly, but *feel.* A disturbance at one node ripples through the network to all others.

3. **Containment.** The Meridian is, at its deepest level, a cage. Beneath the city — far below the foundations, below the bedrock, in a place that is more concept than geography — something sleeps. The oldest texts call it the First Root. It is not a god. It predates the gods. It is the raw, undifferentiated divine potential from which the gods eventually crystallized. It is vast, mindless, and hungry. The Meridian's weight keeps it dormant. If enough nodes fail, the cage opens.

### Architecture

The Meridian's nodes are not evenly distributed. The pattern follows the city's natural geography — water sources, elevated ground, geological features — overlaid with human settlement patterns spanning thousands of years. The result is a web that looks organic from above, almost biological. The ancients mapped it and called the pattern the Ley of Smoke, because the energy lines were first observed as faint trails of vapor rising from the earth on cold mornings.

The central node — the strongest, the oldest — sits beneath what is now the city's main cathedral, built on the foundations of a temple, built on the foundations of a shrine, built on bedrock that has been sacred since before humans had a word for sacred.

## The God-Keepers

### Origin

When the first humans settled near the sacred sites, some of them could feel the gods. Not see them, not speak to them — just a peripheral awareness, a sense of something vast and patient living in the rock beneath their feet. These people became the first priests. Not worshippers — *caretakers.* They didn't pray to the gods. They maintained the sites, cleared the waterways, kept the groves healthy. In return, the land was stable, the water was clean, and the harvests were reliable.

Over millennia, this caretaking became an institution. The temple priests organized into an order dedicated to the preservation of the sacred sites and the monitoring of the Meridian. They called themselves the God-Keepers.

### Structure

The God-Keepers are organized into a hierarchy:

- **The Keeper** — the head of the order in a given city. Currently Thessa. Historically a lifelong appointment chosen by consensus of the senior keepers.
- **Senior Keepers** — those responsible for individual major sacred sites. Usually 5-8 per city, depending on the number of active nodes.
- **Acolytes** — the rank and file. Site maintenance, monitoring, record-keeping. Most god-keepers spend their entire lives as acolytes.

The order is hereditary in practice, though not by rule. The sensitivity to divine presence — the ability to feel the gods — runs in bloodlines. Temple priest families have been producing god-keepers for generations. New blood does appear, but it's rare.

### Modern Era

The god-keepers have no public presence. They operate as a private historical preservation society, a religious order, or simply as eccentrics who care deeply about specific patches of land. The modern world has no framework for what they do, so they don't explain it. They maintain the sites, monitor the Meridian, and keep their knowledge within the order.

The order has fractured over the centuries. Different factions disagree on fundamental questions: Should the gods be preserved at all costs, or allowed to die naturally? Should the Meridian be maintained or allowed to evolve? Should the god-keepers intervene in human development that threatens sacred sites, or accept that the world changes? These disagreements have led to schisms, expulsions, and — in the case of the summoner Selin — radicalization.

### Sacred Law

The god-keepers observe a small body of absolute prohibitions:
1. **Never kill a god.** Even a dying god must be allowed to fade on its own terms.
2. **Never awaken a god-eater.** The ritual exists in the records, but it is forbidden.
3. **Never breach the Meridian.** The network is to be maintained, not modified.
4. **Never reveal the divine to the uninitiated.** The world forgot the gods for a reason. Remembering must be voluntary.

## God-Eaters

### Origin

The god-eaters were not born. They were made.

In the earliest era of the Meridian — long before the god-keepers organized, before temples were built, when the sacred sites were just places where the ground hummed — the gods occasionally died. Not from human interference, but from natural causes: a spring drying up, a grove destroyed by wildfire, a river changing course. When a god died, its energy had to go somewhere. If it dissipated naturally, the transition was gentle. But if the death was sudden or violent, the energy could destabilize the Meridian and wake the thing beneath.

The solution was biological. The first god-keepers — the unorganized, intuitive priest-shamans of prehistory — developed a ritual to create a living vessel for divine energy. They took a human volunteer, subjected them to a transformative rite, and turned them into something that could absorb a dying god's power and hold it safely until it could be redistributed through the Meridian. A shock absorber. A spiritual circuit breaker.

They called these beings *god-eaters* because the process looked, from the outside, like consumption. But the original purpose was preservation. The god-eater was meant to *save* the system, not destroy it.

### The Transformation

The ritual strips away most of the subject's humanity and replaces it with a divine scaffolding capable of containing god-level energy. The process is painful, destructive, and irreversible. The subject loses their memories, their identity, and most of their mortal vulnerability. What remains is a being of immense power — shadow and fire, instinct and hunger — tethered to the mortal world by an anchor.

The transformation is not complete by design. A fully divine god-eater would be indistinguishable from a god — and just as bound to a single location. The human remnant is what allows the god-eater to move, to act, to *choose.* It is also what makes the god-eater dangerous: a being with the power of gods and the unpredictability of humans.

### The Anchor Bond

A god-eater cannot exist untethered. The divine power would consume the mortal shell without a grounding point. The anchor is a living human — specifically, a human with god-keeper bloodline, whose inherent sensitivity to the divine creates a natural resonance with the god-eater's power.

The bond is empathic. Anchor and god-eater feel each other's emotions, sense each other's physical state, and can communicate wordlessly across distance. In its default state, the bond is parasitic — the god-eater's power draws on the anchor's life force, slowly depleting them. An unmanaged bond will kill the anchor within months to a year.

The bond can be stabilized through the Vow of Smoke (see below).

### Physical Traits

A god-eater in its active state exhibits:
- **Thermal anomaly.** Body temperature runs significantly above human normal. The air around them shimmers in cold weather.
- **Shadow response.** Shadows in the god-eater's proximity bend toward them, move independently, and can be directed with conscious effort.
- **Fire affinity.** Flames are attracted to the god-eater and respond to their emotional state. Anger produces heat. Grief produces smoke.
- **Selective invulnerability.** Divine and supernatural forces cannot easily harm a god-eater. Mundane physical trauma — a knife, a bullet, a fall — can. This is the mortal remnant asserting itself.
- **The hunger.** A constant pull toward divine energy. Manageable at baseline, overwhelming in the proximity of a dying god.

## The Vow of Smoke

The Vow of Smoke is the oldest of the binding rites — a spoken promise that restructures the anchor bond from parasitic to symbiotic. The vow must be spoken freely by the god-eater, in the old language, with full knowledge of its consequences.

### What It Does

The vow closes the circuit. In the default bond, energy flows one direction: from the anchor to the god-eater. The vow creates a return path. Energy flows both ways — the god-eater stabilizes the anchor, the anchor stabilizes the god-eater. Neither depletes the other. The bond becomes self-sustaining.

### What It Costs

The vow permanently anchors the god-eater's humanity. The divine scaffolding remains, but it can never fully overwrite the mortal core. This means:
- The god-eater can never achieve full godhood
- The god-eater becomes mortal-vulnerable (they can be hurt, they can age, they can die)
- The god-eater retains the power to absorb and channel divine energy, but cannot be *consumed* by it

In practical terms: the vow trades unlimited potential for a stable existence. Godhood for personhood. Eternity for a lifetime.

### Why It Matters

The vow is the thematic heart of the series. It is a choice — not compelled, not required, freely given — to be human. To accept limitation, mortality, and love as preferable to power, immortality, and isolation. In a universe where gods are dying and the ground is shaking, the most radical act is choosing to be small.

## The City

The city is unnamed by design. It is modern, dense, and old — a place where glass towers stand on foundations that are older than any living language. The geography matters:

- **The Eastern Edge** — industrial, sparse. The abandoned shrine where Ash is found. The old temple ruins buried under a highway overpass.
- **The Harbor District** — port area, warehouses, the threshold between land and sea. A god of passage once lived here.
- **The Historic Quarter** — narrow streets, old churches, the university. Dr. Kaya's domain. Underground passages connect to the oldest parts of the Meridian.
- **The North Quarter** — residential, aging. The vacant lot where new wildflowers grow. A dormant sacred site reawakening.
- **The Central District** — the cathedral, government buildings, the park built over the great temple. The heart of the Meridian.
- **The South Quarter** — the demolished library. Sable's fading domain.

The city's residents have no knowledge of the divine infrastructure beneath them. The sacred sites are parking lots, parks, basements, vacant lots. The gods are forgotten. The Meridian hums beneath the subway lines and no one hears it. This is not a conspiracy of silence — it is the natural result of centuries of urbanization layered over a world that was never meant to be paved.

### Mundane Authority

The city has a standard modern government — mayor, city council, police, fire, emergency services. None of these institutions are aware of the divine. When sacred sites collapse, the explanations are geological. When the Meridian destabilizes, the symptoms are infrastructure failures. The supernatural operates in the gaps of the mundane, and the mundane is very good at not looking into gaps.

Rune exists at the intersection. As a paramedic, he responds to the consequences of divine events — the injuries, the collapses, the panic — without anyone knowing the cause. This dual life is the series' structural engine.

## Bloodlines

The sensitivity to divine presence is hereditary. It manifests as a subtle awareness — a feeling of weight in certain places, an instinct for where the ground is stable, an occasional flash of something vast and patient at the edge of perception. Most carriers never develop it beyond a vague intuition. Those who are identified by the god-keepers are recruited. Those who aren't may live their entire lives as people who are "good with plants" or "always know when it's going to rain."

Rune's family — the Alessi line — descends from Mediterranean temple priests who migrated north centuries ago. The knowledge was lost within two generations. The sensitivity remained. Rune's affinity manifests as his paramedic instinct — the ability to read a patient's state before the monitors do, the sense of when someone is about to crash. He has always assumed this is training. It isn't.

The god-keeper bloodlines are more concentrated. Families like Thessa's have maintained awareness and tradition for generations. The sensitivity in these lines is stronger — they can feel the Meridian directly, sense the health of individual nodes, and detect divine disturbances across the city.

## Timeline

- **Prehistory** — The sacred sites exist. The first gods crystallize. Early humans sense them.
- **Ancient era** — The priest-shamans organize. The god-eater ritual is developed. The Meridian is mapped. The Vow of Smoke is first spoken.
- **Classical era** — The god-keepers formalize into an order. Temples are built at every major node. The Meridian is at full strength.
- **Medieval era** — The first temple destructions. Gods begin to die as sacred sites are demolished for development. The god-keepers go underground.
- **~1,000 years ago** — The last god-eater is created, used, and sealed beneath the eastern shrine when the crisis passes. The ritual is prohibited by sacred law.
- **~30 years ago** — Kaya and Selin are expelled from the god-keepers for heretical beliefs about the gods' natural lifecycle.
- **~6 years ago** — Selin discovers the sealed god-eater beneath the eastern shrine. Begins planning the awakening ritual.
- **~1 year ago** — Selin identifies Rune as a viable anchor through bloodline research.
- **Present day** — Selin performs the ritual. Ash awakens. The series begins.
