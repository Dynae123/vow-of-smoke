# VOW OF SMOKE — Outline QA Report

**Reviewer:** QA Pass
**Date:** 2026-03-06
**Scope:** Full 40-episode outline reviewed against Story Bible and Characters file

## 1. Continuity Issues

### Timeline and Internal Consistency

- **🟡 Warning — Episode 5 vs. Episode 8 day count.** Episode 5's cold open states "Three days since the shrine" (Day 3). Episode 8's cold open states "Day eight." Between them, Episodes 6 and 7 contain events that comfortably fill multiple days each (Thessa's visit, second god death, Kaya's Meridian theory, the nightmare progression). The math works, but only barely. Scriptwriters will need to decide whether the timeline is strict (one episode per day roughly) or elastic. Recommend adding a day marker to episodes 3–10 in the outline so writers don't contradict each other.

- **🟢 Note — "Three dead gods" count in Episode 14.** Ash's hunger is described as "three dead gods' worth of unclaimed power." At this point, gods 1 (Meridian Temple, Ep 4), 2 (sacred grove, Ep 7), and 3 (central park, Ep 10) have died. The spring god in Episode 15 is the fourth target but is still alive during Episode 14. The count is correct but scriptwriters should track carefully since the spring god dies in Ep 15 and the aqueduct god in Ep 20 (dies/goes dormant). By Episode 26, the summoner's message says "four dead gods," which checks out.

- **🔴 Critical — Episode 16 references "the kiss" accelerating the drain.** In Rune's internal at the end of Episode 16, the text reads: "The kiss accelerated the drain. The bond flares spike when emotions run hot." However, the first kiss does not occur until Episode 18 ("Fever"). Episode 16 is the "Threshold" episode where Ash first sleeps in Rune's bed. This is a clear forward-reference error — the narration discusses a kiss that hasn't happened yet. This must be revised. Either remove the kiss reference from Episode 16's internal monologue, or restructure the realization to reference emotional intensity and physical proximity without specifically citing a kiss.

- **🟡 Warning — Kaya's pendant reveal timing.** In Episode 13, Ash notices Kaya's pendant ("his hand goes to something at his throat — a pendant, tucked under his shirt"). In Episode 16, Kaya reveals the pendant's full significance and tells his backstory about being an expelled god-keeper. However, in Episode 9, Kaya already reveals that Rune's bloodline descends from temple priests and that the summoner chose him deliberately, which implies substantial god-keeper inside knowledge. When did Kaya decide to reveal his own god-keeper past? The jump from "evasive about the god-keepers" (Ep 6) to "I was one of them" (Ep 16) works, but Episode 9's exposition dump suggests Kaya is already sharing inside knowledge freely. Consider whether Kaya's Episode 9 info should feel more reluctant, or whether the pendant reveal should come earlier.

- **🟡 Warning — Selin's reveal mechanics.** The Summoner is listed as "identity hidden until Act IV" in the voice cast table. However, Selin is named in the Story Bible timeline ("~30 years ago — Kaya and Selin are expelled") and in the Characters file. The outline itself reveals Selin's identity in Episode 29 (Act III, not Act IV). The voice cast table should be updated to say "Act III" or "Episode 29." More importantly: if the audio drama is released episodically and the Characters file or Story Bible are ever shared publicly (liner notes, website), the Summoner's identity would be spoiled. Flag for the production team.

- **🟢 Note — The aqueduct god's fate.** In Episode 20, the spring beneath the aqueduct "goes still. Not dead — dormant. The god-keepers can preserve it." This god's fate is never revisited. Is it one of the gods that stabilizes in the post-vow Meridian? It should probably be mentioned in the Act IV recovery.

### Character Knowledge Consistency

- **🟡 Warning — Ash's understanding of the bond's lethality.** In Episode 6, the god-keepers tell Rune the bond will kill him ("months, maybe a year"). That night, Ash confirms he can "feel [Rune] dying." But in Episode 9, Ash reacts to the revelation that Rune was deliberately chosen as his anchor with fresh fury, implying he didn't fully understand the stakes. This reads slightly inconsistent — he already knew the bond was killing Rune (Ep 6) but seems newly upset that someone planned this (Ep 9). The distinction (knowing vs. knowing it was deliberate) is valid but should be sharper in script.

- **🟢 Note — When does Mika learn the full truth?** Episode 8: Mika meets Ash, gets a "sanitized version." Episode 13: Rune tells her "all of it." Episode 14: Mika insists on joining the spring operation. The progression works, but Episode 13's revelation is quite late given that Mika is Rune's sister and partner. She's been watching him deteriorate for two weeks without an explanation. This is a character choice, not a plot hole, but scriptwriters should play Mika's frustration in Episodes 8–12.

## 2. Character Arc Tracking

### Rune Alessi
**Arc: Professional savior → someone who'd burn the world for one person**

- **🟢 Note — Strong, well-paced arc.** Rune's progression is the outline's greatest strength. The paramedic framework gives every episode an emotional anchor (no pun intended). His internal monologues carry the series.
- **🟡 Warning — Rune's agency dips in Act III.** Between Episodes 21–26, Rune is primarily reacting: to the summoner's phone calls, to Ash's withdrawal, to Kaya's abduction. He doesn't drive the plot again until Episode 27 (refusing to let Ash sacrifice himself). Consider giving him a proactive beat in Episodes 23–25 — an investigation move, a confrontation, something where Rune pushes the plot forward rather than responding to it.

### Ash
**Arc: Weapon → person. God-eater → someone who chooses humanity.**

- **🟢 Note — Excellent arc.** The progression from hostility (Ep 2) to vulnerability (Ep 7) to love (Ep 18) to sacrifice (Ep 27) to domestic life (Ep 31+) is earned and paced well.
- **🟡 Warning — Ash's memory recovery is underdeveloped.** Fragments surface in Episodes 6, 12, 17, and 30 (when Selin speaks his true name). But between Ep 17 and Ep 30, that's 13 episodes with no memory beats. The memory thread is important — it's what makes the Vow meaningful ("choosing humanity when you remember what was taken"). Consider adding a memory fragment in the Ep 23–26 range.

### Mika Alessi
- **🟡 Warning — Mika disappears for stretches.** After Episode 14 (the spring), Mika is barely present until Episode 20 (the aqueduct). That's 5 episodes. She then disappears again from Episode 22 to 28. She's described as Rune's "grounding wire to the mundane world" and his sister — her absence during the Act III emotional crises (Eps 22–27) is conspicuous. She should have at least a scene in Episodes 22 or 23 and in 25 or 26.

### Dr. Idris Kaya
- **🟢 Note — Well-handled mentor arc.** His god-keeper backstory (Ep 16), his connection to Selin (Ep 25), and his abduction (Ep 25–26) give him a complete arc. His return to teaching (Ep 38–40) is a satisfying resolution.
- **🟡 Warning — Kaya is absent from Episodes 28–30 (the solstice climax) in terms of emotional presence.** He's listed as "handling communications" in Episode 28. Given that Selin is his former colleague and lover, his absence from the confrontation in Episode 29 feels like a missed opportunity. Even a radio-comm reaction from Kaya when Selin is revealed would add weight.

### Thessa
- **🟢 Note — Good antagonist-to-reluctant-ally arc.** Moves from loathing (Ep 6) to grudging reassessment (Ep 10) to needing Ash (Ep 23) to respect (Ep 31). Her Episode 39 moment at the wildflower field is a strong grace note.
- **🟢 Note — Consider giving Thessa one humanizing moment earlier.** She's cold and authoritative through Episode 15. A brief glimpse of the person underneath — even a throwaway line — would make her later softening feel less sudden.

### Sable
- **🟡 Warning — Late introduction, then sporadic.** Sable appears in Episode 24, reappears in Episodes 28, 32, 33, 36, and 39. For a character described as central to "the series' moral crucible" (Ash's relationship with them), they get relatively few scenes. Their biggest function — offering to sacrifice themselves for Rune — happens in their first appearance (Ep 24) and then they mostly serve as exposition support. Consider giving Sable a scene with Ash alone in the Ep 33–34 range, something that pays off the "two beings who should destroy each other finding kinship" thread described in Ep 33.

### Selin
- **🟢 Note — Works well as a late reveal.** The distorted voice appearances (Eps 13, 21, 22) build mystery effectively. The Ep 29 reveal with the personal connection to Kaya is emotionally grounded.
- **🟡 Warning — Selin's post-capture arc (Eps 31–38) may be too sympathetic too fast.** She goes from "primary antagonist who killed four gods" to "writing a complete record as a gift/confession" within a few episodes. The Kaya conversation in Episode 32 is good, but the Ash visit in Episode 38 where she "cracks" might need more resistance. She killed four gods and caused citywide destruction. Redemption should cost more than a cracked expression.

## 3. Pacing Analysis

### Act I (Episodes 1–10): THE BINDING
- **🟢 Note — Excellent pacing.** Each episode escalates naturally. The alternation between intimate apartment scenes (Eps 2, 5, 8) and external crisis scenes (Eps 4, 7, 10) is well-balanced. The romantic tension builds at a pace appropriate for a 5-minute-per-episode format.

### Act II (Episodes 11–20): THE DEEPENING
- **🟡 Warning — Episodes 11–14 are very apartment-heavy.** Four consecutive episodes centered on the apartment with short external excursions. Episode 12 (ritual site) and Episode 14 (the spring) provide some location variety, but the dominant texture is domestic. Consider giving Episode 11 or 13 a stronger external element — a paramedic call, a city scene — to break the rhythm.
- **🟢 Note — The Ep 18 kiss is perfectly placed** at the emotional midpoint of the series (Ep 18 of 40). Good structural instinct.
- **🟢 Note — Episode 20 (the aqueduct) is a strong act break** with genuine stakes and emotional payoff.

### Act III (Episodes 21–30): THE BURNING
- **🔴 Critical — Episodes 25–27 are structurally compressed.** In three episodes (roughly 15 minutes of audio), the outline covers: the summoner's identity investigation (Ep 25), Kaya's abduction and rescue (Eps 25–26), the wall of dead anchors (Ep 26), the solstice deadline (Ep 26), AND the Vow of Smoke (Ep 27). This is the emotional climax of the entire series — Ash choosing humanity — and it's sharing space with a kidnapping subplot and an investigation arc. Recommend either: (a) moving the Kaya abduction earlier (Ep 23–24) to give the Vow its own breathing room, or (b) splitting Episode 27 so the vow gets a full episode without the solstice preparation competing for attention.
- **🟡 Warning — Episodes 29–30 (Selin confrontation and solstice battle) are action-heavy.** Two consecutive episodes of primarily physical conflict. In audio drama, extended action sequences are difficult to make compelling (see Section 6). Ensure at least one of these episodes has a substantial quiet moment mid-action.

### Act IV (Episodes 31–40): THE EMBER
- **🟡 Warning — Act IV has a structural identity crisis.** Episodes 31–33 are epilogue/aftermath. Then Episodes 34–37 introduce a new threat (the siphon) with its own countdown, investigation, and climactic ritual. Then Episodes 38–40 are epilogue again. This creates a mini three-act structure within the final act that may feel like the series ended at Episode 30 and then restarted. The siphon plot is thematically resonant (the bond saves the Meridian), but its introduction in Episode 34 — after three episodes of healing and domesticity — may feel like an unwelcome interruption to the denouement.
- **🟡 Warning — The siphon countdown (Eps 34–37) compresses a full threat arc into 4 episodes (~20 minutes).** Discovery, investigation, impossible choice, workaround, ritual resolution. This matches the pacing of Act III's 10-episode arc but in half the time. It risks feeling rushed.
- **🟢 Note — Episodes 38–40 are a beautiful coda.** The gentle pace, the callbacks, the final rooftop scene — all earned and well-constructed. Protect these at all costs.

## 4. Structural Issues

### Cold Opens
- **🟡 Warning — Cold open variety narrows in Act II.** Acts I and III have varied cold opens: nightmare sequences, sound design pieces, news audio, Kaya lectures, Ash memories. Act II (Eps 11–20) leans heavily on Rune's internal monologue as the cold open device. Episodes 11, 14, 16, 17, 18, and 19 don't have specified cold opens in the outline (the outline shifts to summary format after Act I). Scriptwriters will need to design these. Recommend noting at least the cold open type for each Act II–IV episode to ensure variety.
- **🟢 Note — The nightmare motif (Eps 1, 7, 10, 40) is well-used as a bookend.** The progression from mysterious to revealed (it was Ash's memory reaching across time) is satisfying.

### End Hooks
- **🟢 Note — End hooks escalate effectively through Acts I–III.** Each hook raises the stakes or deepens the emotional question. Strong work.
- **🟡 Warning — Act IV end hooks de-escalate (by design) but some feel repetitive.** Episodes 31, 34, 38, and 39 all end on quiet domestic intimacy between Ash and Rune. This is earned, but four of these in ten episodes may feel samey in audio. Consider varying the texture — an end hook from Ash's POV, or a Meridian-vision, or a moment with Mika.

### Redundancy
- **🟡 Warning — Episodes 21 and 22 feel like they could be one episode.** Both deal with the fallout from the summoner's manipulation revelation. Episode 21 establishes the plot implications (the summoner planned the bond). Episode 22 explores the emotional implications (Ash pulling away). These could potentially be combined, freeing an episode for elsewhere — perhaps giving the Vow of Smoke more room in the Ep 27 range, or expanding the Sable relationship.

## 5. World-Building Gaps

### Story Bible vs. Outline

- **🔴 Critical — The First Root / sleeping god is underspecified in the outline.** The Story Bible establishes that the Meridian is "at its deepest level, a cage" containing "the First Root" — "raw, undifferentiated divine potential... vast, mindless, and hungry." This is a massive cosmological element. In the outline, it appears only in: Episode 29 (Selin reveals the seal), Episode 37 (the circuit reinforces the seal), and Episode 38 (Rune senses it briefly). The First Root is never named as such in the outline — it's called "the sleeping god" or "something beneath." This is fine for the characters' understanding, but scriptwriters need a clear reference for what this entity is and what rules govern it. Recommend adding a brief story bible reference note in Episodes 29 and 37 of the outline.

- **🟡 Warning — God speech rules.** The Story Bible states gods "communicate rarely and with difficulty" and that "a thought that takes a human a second takes a god a season." However, in the outline, gods speak fairly fluently: the spring god delivers a full sentence in Episode 15 ("The anchor holds the chain..."), the aqueduct god has a short dialogue in Episode 20, and the last god speaks clearly in Episode 30. The Bible does note gods speak "in moments of crisis or death," which covers these instances, but the fluency of their dialogue exceeds what the Bible implies. Consider making god-speech more fragmented and labored in script, or updating the Bible to note that dying gods speak more clearly as they shed their geological consciousness.

- **🟡 Warning — Sable's form and the god lifecycle.** The Story Bible says gods "have no fixed physical form" and manifest as "impressions of their origin." Sable is described as "a young person with ancient eyes" who takes "semi-permanent human form" (Ep 33) and brings wine to Rune's apartment (Ep 39). This level of human behavior exceeds what the Bible describes for gods. Either Sable is an exceptional case (which should be stated), or the Bible's rules about god-manifestation need updating. The "gods can't leave their sacred sites" rule is also potentially broken by Sable visiting Rune's apartment, unless the argument is that Sable's site (the demolished library) no longer binds them.

- **🟢 Note — The Vow of Smoke mechanics are consistent.** The outline's depiction matches the Bible's description precisely: freely spoken, old language, closes the parasitic circuit into symbiosis, costs godhood for personhood. Well-aligned.

- **🟢 Note — Selective invulnerability is correctly used.** Ash is hurt by mundane things (pan in Ep 5, bullet in Ep 33) and resistant to divine forces. Consistent with Bible.

### Mechanics Introduced in Outline but Not Grounded in Bible

- **🟡 Warning — The "living conduit" concept (Ep 36–37).** The resolution of the siphon plot involves Ash and Rune becoming "living conduits for the Meridian's power." This is a major escalation of the bond's function that goes well beyond what the Bible describes. The Bible covers the anchor bond and the Vow of Smoke, but "permanent conduits who feel every sacred site and maintain the First Root's cage" is a new tier. This isn't necessarily a problem — the story naturally escalates — but the mechanics should be added to the Bible so scriptwriters have a reference.

- **🟡 Warning — Selin's "harvested god-power."** The outline establishes that Selin has been harvesting divine power from dead gods and carries "the power of four dead gods burning in her veins." The Story Bible doesn't describe any mechanism by which a mortal can absorb divine power without being a god-eater. The god-eater ritual is specifically designed to create a vessel for divine energy. How is Selin doing this? A line of explanation (modified ritual? partial transformation? the siphon device?) would close the gap.

- **🟢 Note — The Kindling (summoner's followers) are not in the Bible or Characters file.** They appear in Episode 23 and recur through Episode 36. They function as a mundane threat vector. Consider adding them to the Bible as a faction entry, even briefly.

## 6. Audio Drama Feasibility

- **🔴 Critical — Episode 30 (the climax) is extremely action-heavy and visually described.** The outline describes: Ash absorbing power from Selin, Rune throwing himself in front of an attack, the bond becoming visible as "a thread of golden fire," the Meridian flaring across the city, Selin being "left mortal." Much of this is described in visual terms. In audio, the scriptwriter will need to convey all of this through sound design and internal monologue. Recommend: (a) keep Rune's internal monologue active during the battle to narrate what's happening, (b) lean heavily on sound design motifs already established (the subsonic thrum, fire crackling, shadow sounds), and (c) consider having characters call out what's happening rather than relying on description.

- **🟡 Warning — Episode 29 (Selin's reveal) involves a physical confrontation in a large space with multiple combatants.** Audio dramas struggle with spatial choreography. Who is where? Who's fighting whom? Scriptwriters should limit the active combatants to 2–3 at any time and use Rune's narration to orient the listener.

- **🟡 Warning — Episode 37 (the circuit ritual) involves simultaneous events across the city** (Thessa anchoring sites, Mika monitoring, Sable lending energy) while Rune and Ash are in the undercrypt. Cross-cutting between locations in audio requires very clear audio signatures per location. Recommend assigning each location a distinct ambient soundscape.

- **🟡 Warning — Bond empathy scenes risk being abstract.** The outline frequently describes emotions flowing through the bond — "Rune feels Ash's shame," "Ash feels Rune's grief." These are described as felt sensations, not heard ones. In audio, the scriptwriter will need to externalize these — through Rune's narration, through physical reactions (gasping, stumbling), or through a distinctive sound design element for bond-transfer moments. Recommend establishing a consistent "bond sound" early (Episode 2 or 3) and using it throughout.

- **🟢 Note — The paramedic framework is excellent for audio.** Radio calls, dispatch audio, sirens, medical terminology — these are inherently audio-friendly elements that ground the supernatural in familiar soundscapes.

- **🟢 Note — The 5-minute format is well-suited to the outline's structure.** Most episodes have a clean three-beat structure (cold open, main action, end hook) that fits the runtime. Episodes 29 and 30 may need to run slightly long.

## 7. Missing Elements

### Characters Mentioned but Underdeveloped

- **🟡 Warning — The previous anchors.** Episode 26 reveals a wall of photographs — previous anchors who died. Episode 38 mentions Selin's record includes their names and that "Kaya ensures their families are contacted." This is a significant emotional thread (Rune wasn't the first; others died) that's introduced in one episode and resolved in a single sentence twelve episodes later. Consider giving Rune a moment to sit with this — reading the names, processing what his survival means against their deaths. Even one scene in Episodes 31–33 would do.

- **🟡 Warning — Dispatch and EMT Calls.** The Characters file lists Dispatch and EMT Calls as recurring one-off voices. After Episode 1, dispatch appears only briefly (Ep 1 off-duty call). For an audio drama about a paramedic, the ambient world of emergency services should be a more consistent presence — at least a dispatch call or ambulance scene every few episodes to maintain the dual-life texture.

### Plot Threads

- **🟡 Warning — The god-keeper traitor.** Episode 16: Kaya determines "someone within the god-keepers is working with the summoner." Episode 25: the investigation narrows. But the traitor is never explicitly identified. Selin is a former god-keeper, not a current one. The Kindling have followers, but the specific "inside" traitor who had access to god-keeper sacred law and ritual knowledge is never named or caught. Is it implied that Selin herself is the source of inside knowledge (from her time as a keeper)? If so, this should be made explicit. If there's an actual mole, they need to be found.

- **🟢 Note — Rune's nightmares.** The nightmare thread (burning temple, hand slipping) is set up in Episode 1 and resolved in Episode 40 (it was Ash's memory reaching through time). This is clean and satisfying. Well done.

- **🟢 Note — Ash's true name.** Selin speaks it in Episode 30. Ash remembers it in Episode 38. He doesn't share it with Rune ("Someday. When the last scar fades"). This is a deliberate open thread for a potential Season 2 or simply a graceful loose end. Works either way.

- **🟡 Warning — The sleeping god / First Root.** This is established as a world-ending threat contained by the Meridian. After the circuit ritual (Ep 37), Episode 38 has Rune sense it briefly and Ash confirm "the seal holds." But this entity has been positioned as the ultimate threat beneath everything. If there is no Season 2 planned, this thread works as cosmic atmosphere. If there IS a Season 2 planned, it's good setup. But if the series is meant to be self-contained, the First Root may feel like a Chekhov's gun that never fires. Clarify the intent and adjust the Episode 38 beat accordingly — either close it more firmly or lean into the sequel hook.

### Foreshadowing

- **🟢 Note — The pendant (Ep 13 → Ep 16) pays off well.**
- **🟢 Note — The rooftop (Ep 8 → Ep 40) callback is excellent structural bookending.**
- **🟡 Warning — Kaya's line in Episode 9 cold open** ("what the myths never explain is why the anchor survives at all") is a great piece of foreshadowing for the Vow of Smoke. But the answer — the vow — doesn't come until Episode 19 (Kaya's revelation) and Episode 27 (the vow itself). That's an 18-episode gap in a 5-min-per-episode format. Consider a brief reminder of this question around Episode 15 or 16.

## Summary

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong, emotionally grounded outline with a clear thematic spine and well-paced romantic arc. The BL elements are organic to the story rather than grafted on — the bond mechanic makes the romantic progression inseparable from the plot, which is exactly what this format needs.

**Priority Fixes (Critical):**
1. Episode 16 references a kiss that hasn't happened yet — revise the internal monologue.
2. Episodes 25–27 are structurally compressed — the Vow of Smoke needs more breathing room.
3. Episode 30's climactic battle needs audio-feasibility notes for the scriptwriter.
4. The First Root needs to be referenced by name in the outline where relevant, with Bible cross-references.

**Recommended Improvements (Warnings):**
1. Restore Mika's presence in the episodes where she drops out (Eps 15–19, 22–27).
2. Address the god-keeper traitor thread — clarify whether Selin is the inside source or if there's an unresolved mole.
3. Add audio-production notes for bond empathy scenes (establish a consistent sound motif).
4. Consider combining Episodes 21–22 to free space for the Vow episode.
5. Ground Selin's ability to harvest divine power in the Story Bible.
6. Develop Act IV's siphon subplot pacing — it currently introduces and resolves a full threat arc in 4 episodes.
7. Add an Ash memory-fragment beat somewhere in Episodes 23–26.
8. Clarify Sable's ability to leave their sacred site and take sustained human form.

**Strengths to Preserve:**
- Rune's internal monologue voice is the series' greatest asset. Protect it.
- The paramedic framework provides both emotional grounding and audio-friendly texture.
- The Vow of Smoke thematic throughline (choosing humanity over power) is clear and consistent.
- The romantic pacing — from fear to fascination to need to love — is excellently calibrated for 40 episodes.
- The rooftop bookend (Ep 8 → Ep 40) is a perfect structural choice.
- Episode 16 ("Threshold") is the outline's best episode on paper — tender, surprising, earned. Protect every beat of it (after fixing the kiss reference).
