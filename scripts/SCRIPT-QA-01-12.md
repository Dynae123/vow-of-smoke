### Episode 1: "First Response"

**Cold open:** Strong. The nightmare-to-siren cut is clean and immediately establishes the dual-world premise. The voice calling "Rune" through fire is effective audio drama.

**Character voice:**
- RUNE spoken: Excellent. Clipped, professional, warm with the patient. "Tell her it's my fault. I'm very persuasive" is perfect Rune humor. Deflection with Mika ("Bad sleep. That's all.") matches the speech patterns doc exactly.
- RUNE internal: Present tense, zero distance, strong closers. "He says my name like he's losing me" is a good gut-punch opener. The apartment monologue ("Home. Which is a generous word for it.") establishes voice efficiently.
- MIKA: Nailed. "You look like you haven't slept in a month" -- observes and states, never asks "are you okay?" per the rules. "He's really not" is dry and sisterly.
- ASH: Only one word ("...You.") but it lands. Low, raw, accusatory. Correct.

**The gap:** Well maintained. Rune's internal is rich and vulnerable; his spoken voice is clipped and practical. The distance between "He says my name like he's losing me" and "Yeah. I'm up." is the show's engine working.

**Pacing:** Good for a pilot. Five scenes, clear escalation. The routine call grounds us before the shrine shatters everything. Feels like 5 minutes.

**End hook:** The scars igniting, the lock behind the sternum, "...You." -- strong. Sets up everything.

- 🟢 **Note:** The SFX line "[SFX: eyes opening -- not a sound, but the shift in the man's breathing]" is a production note, not a sound cue. Flag for sound designer to interpret.
- 🟢 **Note:** "Something in my chest won't let me" (line 172) -- "something in" construction. Isolated here, fine. Watch for accumulation.

### Episode 7: "The Meridian"

**Cold open:** Sound design -- dogs barking in unison then stopping. Great audio moment. Varied from the previous opens.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: "A death." / "South side. The grove." -- clinical shorthand. One-word answers when the content is enormous. Perfect.
- ASH: "Do not let me stay here." -- the reversal. The predator asking to be pulled away from prey. This is the character turn the episode needs and it's earned.
- ASH: "Why are you not afraid of me?" -- note the full "are you not" rather than "aren't you." Correct.
- RUNE spoken: "I am." -- two words. The bravest line in the episode. Matches the rule: "When Rune tells the truth about something that matters, the sentence is naked."

**The gap:** The tea scene is the gap in miniature. Internal: "Because being afraid of someone and being afraid for someone are two different things." Spoken: nothing. He drinks tea. The monologue earns its weight because the spoken Rune withholds it. This is the show working.

**Pacing:** Excellent variety. Action scene (the grove), quiet domestic scene (the tea), phone exposition (Kaya), nightmare sequence. The episode breathes.

**End hook:** Ash in the doorway. "You were dreaming about me." Dream-face reveal. Strong -- plot hook AND emotional hook.

- 🟡 **Warning:** "The hum. Low. Steady. The sound of someone standing guard in a doorway and pretending it's something else." -- this is the fifth "[SFX: the hum. Low/Steady/Patient...]" construction across the scripts. The closing SFX lines are becoming a formula: "The hum" + two adjectives + "The sound of [metaphorical description]." Vary the structure. Some episodes should end on dialogue, some on action, some on silence without the hum editorial.

**Hum closing pattern across episodes:**
- Ep 1: "The hum. Low. Steady. The sound of a bond that will not break."
- Ep 2: "The hum. Steady. Patient. The sound of something that has no intention of stopping."
- Ep 4: "The hum. Low. Steady. The sound of a bond that is learning new tricks..."
- Ep 6: "The hum. Steady. Patient. The apartment around them..."
- Ep 7: "The hum. Low. Steady. The sound of someone standing guard..."

This is a pattern that needs breaking. Five of the first seven episodes end with essentially the same construction.

## CROSS-EPISODE SUMMARY

### 1. CHARACTER VOICE FIDELITY

**RUNE spoken:** Consistently strong. Clipped, clinical, deflecting. Dry humor lands well. Only one notable overstep (Ep 6, line 84 -- too eloquent for spoken register).

**RUNE internal:** The Raw Nerve voice is the show's greatest asset. Present tense maintained throughout. Gut-punch closers are earned more often than not. The medical metaphor as poetry is consistent and effective.

**ASH:** Excellent across all 12 episodes. Zero contractions verified by search. Archaic-formal register maintained. Short declaratives. The voice evolution is tracked -- he's slightly less hostile by Ep 12 but hasn't softened his syntax yet. One concern: Ep 10's speech to Thessa is long for early Ash, though earned by context.

**MIKA:** Strong. She sounds like a real paramedic partner, not a plot device. Her best episodes are 4, 8, and 11. She asks what others avoid, observes without judgment, and translates the supernatural into practical terms. No violations found.

**KAYA:** Consistent academic register. "You understand" verbal tic deployed at correct frequency (8 times across 12 episodes). Tends toward exposition dumps -- see specific episode notes. His information-withholding is well-tracked through Rune's internal observations.

**THESSA:** Appears in Eps 5, 6, and 10. Zero contractions. Uses "it" for Ash in Eps 5-6, hasn't shifted to "he" yet -- correct per timeline. Cold, authoritative, but not one-note: her reaction to the park sinkhole in Ep 10 shows cracks. More screen time needed to develop dimension -- she's currently functional but thin.

### 4. THE GAP

**Overall: Strong. This is the show's best-maintained element.**

The distance between what Rune says and thinks is consistently preserved. Best examples:
- Ep 7: Internal ("afraid of vs. afraid for") -- spoken: drinks tea. Says nothing.
- Ep 9: Internal (the monster cares) -- spoken: "We'll figure it out."
- Ep 11: Internal (the scar, the thumb, the denial cracking) -- spoken: nothing.

**One violation:**
- 🟡 Ep 6, line 84: "I'm choosing to not hand over a person to people who don't think he's one." This is internal-register language coming out of Rune's mouth. Spoken Rune doesn't make thesis statements. The gap narrows here -- he's saying exactly what he's thinking, and in words that are too polished. Rework to something blunter.

**No instances of redundancy** (internal monologue restating what the dialogue already conveyed) were found. The internal narration consistently adds dimension rather than echoing.

### 5. DIALOGUE NATURALNESS

## OVERALL ASSESSMENT

The scripts are strong. The voice fidelity is excellent -- each character sounds like themselves, the gap between Rune's spoken and internal registers is consistently maintained, and the Raw Nerve style delivers. The AI-tell concerns are real but are polishing issues, not structural problems. The biggest risk is pattern fatigue: the hum closings, the sternum references, the beat overuse, and the emotional-hook endings create a rhythmic sameness that careful revision can fix.

The show's engine -- the distance between what Rune says and what he thinks -- is running well. The episodes where it works best (7, 9, 11, 12) are the show at its peak. The one place it falters (Ep 6, line 84) is an easy fix.
