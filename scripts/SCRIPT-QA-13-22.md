### Episode 13: "Vital Signs"

**Cold open:** Routine call going wrong. Ambulance bay, physical collapse. Strong shift from the supernatural-heavy Ep 12 — grounds us back in Rune's day job before pulling the floor out. Good variety.

**Character voice:**
- RUNE spoken: Excellent. "Bad sleep. That's all" has been retired — Mika cuts him off before he can reach for it. "The bond is parasitic" is the clinical register reframing the supernatural. When he says "Week one," the two-word admission is devastatingly on-spec for Rune-tells-the-truth mode.
- RUNE internal: "I've rehearsed four different versions of 'it's nothing' and none of them are going to work" — the self-awareness of a bad liar. Strong.
- MIKA: Best Mika writing in the series so far. "Not 'look.' Not 'bad sleep, that's all.' Not 'long story.'" — she dismantles his deflection toolkit by naming every piece. "What do you need" lands exactly per the speech patterns doc. "Non-negotiable" repeated twice — this is Mika triaging, and the repetition is earned.
- ASH: The Kaya confrontation is sharp. "You are asking questions the way a surgeon asks where it hurts" — precise, accusatory, archaic syntax. Zero contractions. "What else do you want. What the stone smelled like" — the rawness under the formality, correct.
- KAYA: The god-of-a-paved-over-river story is his best scene. "You understand" appears once. The pendant reveal feels earned. "Love seems inadequate and accurate in equal measure" — this is Kaya at his most personal, and the line walks the boundary of his academic register without violating it.
- THE SUMMONER: First direct appearance. "The spring dies in two days" — conditional structure, no wasted words, distorted but clear. "I gave him to you so you would soften him" — clinical intimacy, proprietary tone, exactly per speech patterns doc. Distinct voice from all others. Good.

**The gap:** Strong. Internal Rune processes the summoner's call with dread and rage; spoken Rune reports it to Ash in clipped factual sentences. "The summoner called" — four words against a paragraph of internal processing.

**Pacing:** Four scenes, clean escalation. Ambulance collapse, break room disclosure, Kaya/Ash scene, summoner call. Each scene serves a different function. Feels right for five minutes.

**End hook:** "I would follow him into the ground" — emotional hook, but the plot machinery (summoner call, two-day timeline) gives it teeth. Balanced.

- 🟢 **Note:** The closing monologue (lines 315-319) uses a threat-list structure ("my blood doesn't clot right...the summoner knows my name...the god-eater is the most dangerous thing"), echoing the three-threat pattern flagged in the 1-12 QA. However, this instance resolves into a personal declaration rather than the previous formula's "list then pivot to the intimate." The list is longer (four items) and the closer ("I would follow him into the ground") is active, not observational. Marginally distinct. Monitor but not a repeat offense.

### Episode 14: "The Spring"

**Cold open:** Battle planning at the kitchen table. Ensemble scene, voices overlapping. Good variety from the two-character opens of recent episodes.

**Character voice:**
- MIKA: "You're my partner. I don't leave my partner. That's it. That's the whole argument. Move on." — a pulled-from-the-speech-patterns-doc line that earns its place. She packed the kit before she arrived. Mika never hedges.
- ASH: "Their caution is not unreasonable" about the god-keepers' fear of him — flat, honest, zero contractions. "Ask me something I can answer" (re: can he hold the hunger) — devastating in its precision. Zero contractions throughout.
- KAYA: Measured, map-focused. "The timing" paragraph is expository but broken up by Ash's interruptions. Better-managed exposition than the Kaya dumps flagged in Eps 3-4.
- THESSA (via proxy): Referenced but not present in dialogue. Her acolytes appear in Scene 4 — silent, watchful. Their presence works as atmospheric shorthand.

**The bathroom scene (Scene 2):** The series' first real romantic set piece. The physical setup is excellent for audio — small space reverb, porcelain cracking, the faucet. Ash's fingers on Rune's jaw is properly staged: slow, light, giving the exit. Rune's internal going silent ("for the first time since the shrine my internal voice stops talking") is a strong choice — the Raw Nerve narrator unable to narrate. The Mika-knock interruption is well-timed: not a cliche interruption but an earned one (she's there for the bloodwork, which is its own ticking clock).

**The gap:** The bathroom scene tests the gap in a new way. For once, Rune's internal and external are both silent — the gap closes not through eloquence but through the inability of either register to speak. This is effective and novel.

**Pacing:** Good. Three distinct tempos: strategy room (fast, ensemble), bathroom (slow, two-person), tunnel descent (building tension, atmospheric). Feels right for five minutes.

**End hook:** Chanting from below + "the ritual has begun" — strong plot hook. Varied from the emotional hooks of recent episodes. The callback to the jaw-touch ("His fingers on my jaw. I can still feel it") layers emotional residue over the plot urgency. Good.

- 🟢 **Note:** "He says it flat. No edge." (line 48) is the "he says it flat" pattern flagged in the 1-12 QA (14 instances). This is the first appearance in the 13-22 batch. Acceptable at this frequency.

### Episode 15: "Undertow"

**Cold open:** Continuation from the Ep 14 cliffhanger — descending the last switchback. Momentum-driven. Good.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: "I am not your weapon" — the episode's centerpiece line. Four words, said to the summoner, to himself, to the ritual. Correct register: devastating, no preamble. "Ask me something I can answer" (from Ep 14) was the setup; "I am not your weapon" is the payoff. The voice direction note (said to everything at once) is smart for the audio director.
- THE SUMMONER: "Disappointing. But not unexpected." — perfect. Two sentences, calm, already recalculating. The Summoner never threatens, only informs.
- SPRING GOD: "The anchor holds the chain. But the chain holds the anchor, too." — pulled from Sable's example lines in the speech patterns doc. This is reuse of a specific image, but spoken by a different god. If Sable uses this exact line later, it will read as a repetition.
- MIKA: "That's your plan. Yelling." / "My plans have been getting worse since the shrine." — the Rune/Mika dynamic is excellent under pressure. She remains unimpressed, clinical, darkly funny.
- GOD-KEEPER ACOLYTE: "The Keeper will want a report. Three ritualists are in our custody." — clipped, formal, impersonal. Distinct from other voices. Good.

**The hand scene (Scene 5):** Ash takes Rune's hand in front of Mika and the acolytes. The script handles this well — not a dramatic declaration but a physical need. "He takes my hand and holds on like the earth is still trying to pull him down." Mika's silence is better than dialogue here: "The deliberate silence of someone choosing to see and not speak."

**The gap:** Maintained. The hand-holding scene is physical, not verbal — Rune's internal processes it richly, but spoken Rune says nothing. Ash says nothing. The scene earns its silence.

**Pacing:** Five scenes — approach, battle, choice, god speaks, aftermath/ascent. This is the most action-heavy episode in the series and it moves well. The battle scene is brief enough for audio (the chaos is conveyed through SFX and short exchanges, not extended description). Good audio drama instincts.

**End hook:** "One of them is talking" — plot hook. Clean. The shift from the emotional weight of the hand-holding to the intel-gathering pragmatism is a good tonal whiplash.

- 🟡 **Warning:** SPRING GOD line "The anchor holds the chain. But the chain holds the anchor, too." (line 169) is identical to a Sable example line in SPEECH-PATTERNS.md. If Sable speaks this line later in the series, it will read as a repetition across divine characters rather than a thematic echo. Either flag this as intentionally shared (divine beings sharing a perspective) or differentiate the phrasing now.

### Episode 16: "Threshold"

**Cold open:** The aftermath — Rune barely making it through the door. Physical fragility. No supernatural element in the open. Good variety.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: The measuring cup. The soup heated with his hands. The paramedic manual. This is Ash's best episode. Every detail communicates the same thing: a being who has never cared for anyone, inventing care from first principles. "I could not locate a glass." / "If you stop breathing, I will need to know." — the first line is domestic comedy, the second is devastating. Zero contractions. Archaic syntax maintained under vulnerability. Sentences stay short: "Eat." / "Where." / "This will serve." All correct.
- KAYA: The pendant story, told in full here (expanded from the brief version in Ep 13). "I study what I have lost and call it scholarship" — strong self-awareness, academic register preserved. His exposition about the bond mechanics (active use accelerates the drain) is delivered as context, not lecture, because Rune is already symptomatic. The stakes make the information urgent rather than academic.
- RUNE spoken: "Stay." — the one-word line that carries the scene. Matches the speech patterns rule: "When Rune tells the truth about something that matters, the sentence is naked." "I'm making space" adds practical action to the emotional word. Peak Rune.
- RUNE internal: The bond-breathing discovery in Scene 4 is the series' most important bond-mechanic moment, and it's earned through observation rather than exposition. "Proximity isn't the variable" is clinical Rune applying the paramedic analytical lens to the supernatural. Good.

**The gap:** Scene 4 is the gap at its most compressed — Rune says "Stay" and then the entire internal monologue processes what that word means. The spoken word is tiny; the internal consequence is enormous. Perfect.

**Pacing:** Four scenes, clean slow-burn rhythm. Physical aftermath, caregiving, Kaya visit, nighttime scene. The domestic pacing is appropriate post-action. Feels right for five minutes.

**End hook:** "He's here. He's staying. And the bond isn't burning. It's breathing." — emotional hook, but the "breathing" discovery is also a mechanical revelation (the bond responds to intent, not just proximity). Dual-function closer.

- 🟡 **Warning:** "Doesn't know what to do with his hands" (line 32) — the "doesn't know what to do with [noun]" construction was flagged in the 1-12 QA (3 instances, instruction to keep one and rework others). This is the first instance in the 13-22 batch. It appears again in Ep 19, line 134 ("doesn't know what to do with"). Two instances in 10 episodes is within the limit (max 1 per 5 eps would be ideal), but the construction is still present. The Ep 16 version ("hands") is weaker than the Ep 19 version (which is about the sentence Rune said); consider cutting the Ep 16 instance and replacing with a more specific physical description.

- 🟢 **Note:** "I file the distinction away like a clinical note" (line 328). "I file this away" was flagged in the 1-12 QA at 2 uses with instruction "don't add more." This is a third instance. The variant ("like a clinical note") adds specificity, but the construction is accumulating. Replace with a different internalization gesture.

### Episode 18: "Fever"

**Cold open:** Locker room. Blood draw. The mirror. The physical decline narrated through medical specifics. Good — grounded, clinical, urgent.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: "This is not the bond. I know what the bond feels like. This is me." — the midpoint thesis. Ash at his most vulnerable: splitting himself into bond-component and person, insisting the person is the one speaking. "Not the anchor. You." — the repetition-emphasis pattern from the speech patterns doc ("Ash emphasizes by stripping away everything that isn't the point"). Executed perfectly. Zero contractions. The formal syntax ("I know what the bond feels like") under extreme emotional pressure — correct per the rule that archaic register returns under stress.
- ASH: "I need you alive." / "I need you." — the escalation from conditional to unconditional across the scene. "Need" appears three times (lines 162, 176, 200) — the repetition is Ash's emphasis tool, documented in the speech patterns. Earned.
- RUNE spoken: "We shouldn't —" — two words and a dash. The trailing off, the inability to complete the sentence. Matches "Trailing off: Spoken Rune trails off with em dashes when emotion catches him." The "shouldn't" is the last attempt at the professional register before it collapses.
- MIKA: "You're sitting down." — the deadpan correction when Rune says he stood up too fast. Peak Mika under pressure: clinical observation delivered with the precision of a scalpel.

**The kiss (Scene 3):** This is the question from the brief — does it land? Is it earned?

**Yes.** The build from Ep 14 (jaw touch) through Ep 16 (staying in bed, the bond breathing) through Ep 17 ("that felt like this") creates a genuine escalation. Each episode adds a layer: physical proximity (14), chosen cohabitation (16), emotional declaration from Ash (17), confrontation about mortality (18). By the time Ash says "This is not the bond. This is me," the audience has been given enough evidence to believe him. The kiss is not a surprise — it's a release. The script handles it well for audio: the SFX cues are intimate and specific (back against counter, maps rustling, hands glowing), and Rune's internal narration is stripped to fragments ("His mouth." / "Smoke."). The Raw Nerve narrator breaking into fragments is earned — this is the second time the internal voice fails (after the bathroom in Ep 14), and both times it's because the body has overridden the narrator.

The aftermath (Scene 4) is equally important: they separate. Ash is on the couch, not the bed. The kiss did not resolve anything — it opened something. The ambiguity is preserved.

**The gap:** The kiss scene is the gap's most extreme test. Rune's internal breaks into fragments during the kiss, then rebuilds in the aftermath. Spoken Rune says almost nothing: "We shouldn't —" and "I don't care." The internal voice does the heavy processing: "the line was never there." The gap holds even at the moment of maximum intimacy.

**Pacing:** Four scenes, strong escalation. Clinical open, confrontation, kiss, aftermath. The energy peaks in Scene 3 and Scene 4 provides the necessary deceleration. Feels right for five minutes.

**End hook:** "I think the line was never there." — the philosophical conclusion to the bond-versus-person question. Emotional hook with thematic weight.

- 🟡 **Warning:** The closing monologue (lines 304-326) deploys a modified three-threat structure: "The god-keepers say...Kaya says...Mika says..." (line 304). This is the exact pattern flagged in the 1-12 QA (Eps 9, 10, 12). The text is: "The god-keepers say the bond will eat me alive. Kaya says the active use accelerates the drain. Mika says my platelets are dropping." Three authority figures, three warnings, then the intimate pivot ("And the kiss didn't drain"). This must be reworked. The 1-12 QA explicitly flagged this as a recurring formula that should not recur.

- 🟢 **Note:** "I need you" appears three times in Ash's dialogue across the scene (lines 162, 176, 200). The repetition is earned and matches his documented emphasis pattern. However, verify with the voice director that the actor varies the delivery across all three — each should land differently (the first as escaped truth, the second as conviction, the third as naked plea).

### Episode 19: "Anchor"

**Cold open:** The morning after the kiss. Grey light, cold coffee, Ash at the counter rehearsing an exit speech. Emotional open, but the content (Ash threatening to leave) gives it tension. Good.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: "The bond will kill you faster now" — the opening salvo. Clinical, flat, rehearsed. "Every clinical metric your sister is tracking — the hemoglobin, the clotting factors, the inflammatory markers" — Ash has absorbed medical language from Rune's world. This is the mid-series speech absorption documented in the Voice Evolution Tracker. He's using Rune's vocabulary to argue against Rune. Good character detail.
- ASH: "For a being that was never meant to love. You can say it." — completing Kaya's sentence. Direct, no preamble, truth like a stone. The word "love" spoken aloud for the first time in the series — by Ash, not Rune. Per the speech patterns doc, "Rune NEVER says 'I love you' directly in early or mid acts. The word arrives late or never." Ash is the one who names it. This is the right choice.
- RUNE: "Don't walk away from me. Don't decide for me." — pulled from the speech patterns example lines. Under pressure, imperatives, sentences collapse to fragments. "I am dying anyway" — the truth, naked, no qualifiers. "I know what I'm dying for" — the line that earns the entire episode.
- RUNE: "That's his choice. Not mine." — refusing to ask Ash to speak the Vow. This is the most important character beat in the episode. It would be easy to have Rune push for the Vow as survival instinct. Instead, he protects Ash's agency at the cost of his own life. This is Rune at his best.
- KAYA: The Vow reveal is handled correctly per the brief. Kaya reveals mechanics (permanent humanity, loss of selective invulnerability, mortal-vulnerable) — not existence, which Rune already knew. "The situation has evolved" — and the line about Ash wearing Rune's sweatshirt is Kaya at his most perceptive and human. The academic seeing the love and naming it. Good.

**The Vow reveal:** Does the scene handle the "mechanics, not existence" correctly? **Yes.** Rune's internal monologue confirms he already knew about the Vow (line 314: "I knew the vow existed. I didn't know the cost"). Kaya's dialogue explicitly frames the new information as the secondary texts, the sealed records, the cost. The distinction is clean.

**The gap:** The embrace in Scene 2 — "I don't want to break the bond" is one of the rare moments where Rune's spoken voice matches his internal voice. The gap closes deliberately, and the script acknowledges this is significant. It's a truthful moment per the speech patterns: "When Rune tells the truth about something that matters, the sentence is naked."

**Pacing:** Five scenes. Morning confrontation, embrace, preparation, Kaya call, aftermath. This is the densest episode in the batch in terms of emotional and plot payload (Ash tries to leave, they embrace, Vow mechanics revealed, Ash says "love," Rune refuses to ask). It is on the edge of too much for five minutes.

- 🟡 **Warning:** This episode carries three major emotional beats (the attempted departure, the embrace, the Vow reveal) plus two major plot beats (Vow mechanics, summoner's endgame confirmed). For a ~5-minute audio episode, this is dense. The embrace scene (Scene 2) could be tightened — the post-embrace internal monologue (lines 140-144) restates what the scene already conveyed. Consider cutting lines 140-144 and letting the city-waking ambient carry the emotional weight.

- 🟢 **Note:** "You are the most infuriating person I have ever encountered in a thousand years of existence" (line 338) — Ash's longest spoken sentence in the series. It's earned by the moment (post-Vow reveal, post-Rune refusing to ask). But verify it doesn't set a precedent for extended Ash speeches. His power remains in what he doesn't say.

### Episode 20: "The Aqueduct"

**Cold open:** The descent. Full team. Multiple voices. Action-focused. The strongest ensemble open in the series.

**Character voice:**
- ASH: "I was made for a lot of things. I am choosing something else." — spoken alone, with no audience. This is the critical test: does Ash's refusal hold when Rune is not present? Yes. The line is honest — "I am choosing" rather than "I am not" — and it acknowledges the hunger rather than denying it. Zero contractions. Formal syntax under maximum stress.
- ASH: "I just wanted something else more." (to Thessa) — devastating in its simplicity. Does not look at Rune. The "something else" is unspoken. Correct: Ash does not babble or over-explain.
- THE AQUEDUCT GOD: "The water remembers many things. It will remember this. A god-eater who chose to be hungry." — the god's voice is distinct from the spring god: warmer, more conversational, older. The water-imagery is consistent. The curiosity of encountering something unexpected is well-conveyed.
- THESSA: "You held." — two words. The formal register breaking into something approaching respect. This is the documented shift: "contempt softening to grudging respect." No contractions. Correct.
- MIKA: "Pulse is there. Slow but steady. Temperature dropping — that's new." — paramedic triage applied to a god-eater. Clinical, professional, treating the insane as manageable.
- THE SUMMONER (end stinger): "He resisted. Interesting. Then we will need to give him something he cannot refuse." — the Summoner's voice is chillingly calm. "Interesting" is the most unsettling word in the episode. Correct: the Summoner does not change register under pressure.

**End hook:** The Summoner's stinger — "Then we will need to give him something he cannot refuse" — is the strongest plot hook in the batch. The hard audio cut from the intimate temple-kiss scene to the empty, cold summoner room is excellent audio drama.

- 🟢 **Note:** The temple kiss (Ash's lips on Rune's temple) in Scene 6 is perfectly placed — after the action, in the silence, without urgency. It contrasts the kitchen kiss (desperate, urgent) with something quieter and more certain. The progression from Ep 14 (jaw touch) to Ep 18 (kiss) to Ep 20 (temple) tracks a clear arc from question to declaration to settled fact.

### Episode 21: "Aftershock"

**Cold open:** City falling apart — news audio, sinkholes, infrastructure failure. The macro-consequences of divine death. Strong variety — the world-building reminds listeners of stakes beyond the central relationship.

**Character voice:**
- MIKA: "Since the aqueduct. Or since whatever happened the night before the aqueduct that you haven't told me about and I'm not asking." — Mika at her best. Sees everything, names only what she chooses to. "What are we doing? What is the plan?" — direct, no hedging.
- THE SUMMONER: The phone call in Scene 3 is the summoner's most substantial appearance. "I gave him to you so you would soften him" (repeated from Ep 13) lands harder in full context. "The emotional attachment was necessary for the completion ritual" — the clinical explanation of love as mechanism. "A god-eater who would burn the world for one person" — this is the summoner's best line: it inverts the show's thesis (love as salvation) into the summoner's thesis (love as weapon). Correct register: calm, informing, no threats.
- ASH: The rooftop scene (Scene 5) pushes Ash's voice in two directions simultaneously — the archaic register resurfacing under stress ("Every variable accounted for") and the emotional content breaking through anyway. "Of course I am scared. I am a weapon and you are the thing that makes me vulnerable" — this is Ash's longest, most emotionally complex speech. Formal syntax ("Of course I am") under genuine distress. It works because the content matches the formality — he's making an argument, not confessing. The confession is underneath.
- RUNE: "They set the stage. They didn't write the lines." — Rune's counter-argument is his best spoken line in the batch. Short, direct, metaphorical in a way spoken Rune rarely allows himself. This borders on the Ep 6 concern (too eloquent for spoken register), but the metaphor is simple enough — stage/lines — to feel like something a person would actually say in an argument. Acceptable.
- KAYA: "Consistent with the texts" — Rune's internal observation ("The most Kaya sentence ever spoken") is the show's humor at its best. Self-aware without breaking the fourth wall.

**Pacing:** This is the merged episode (per the brief) and the density concern is real. Six scenes: city collapse montage, Kaya's discovery, summoner phone call, aftermath, rooftop confrontation, car ride to safe house. The emotional payload is enormous — the summoner's weaponization reveal, Ash pulling away, the rooftop argument, the safe house attack. Each scene works individually, but the cumulative effect is relentless.

- 🔴 **Critical:** This episode is too dense for ~5 minutes of audio. Six scenes with three major emotional confrontations (summoner call, aftermath, rooftop) plus an action trigger (safe house attack) will play at approximately 7-8 minutes or will require cutting that loses essential content. **Recommendation:** Split the car scene (Scene 6) off as the cold open of Ep 22, or compress Scenes 1-2 (city montage and Kaya discovery) into a single scene. The rooftop confrontation (Scene 5) cannot be cut — it is the emotional climax of the summoner's revelation arc.

**The gap:** The rooftop scene is the gap under the most pressure it's faced. Rune's internal processes the weaponization horror; his spoken voice argues against Ash's withdrawal with logic and feeling simultaneously. The gap narrows here — Rune is more eloquent than usual in speech — but the circumstances justify it. He's fighting for the relationship with everything he has, including words.

**End hook:** "None of it is resolved and all of it is happening and we are here." — emotional hook, but the safe house attack provides the plot urgency. Balanced.

- 🟡 **Warning:** Ash has zero contractions in Eps 13-20, as expected for mid-series. In Ep 21, he still has zero contractions in dialogue. The speech patterns doc says "first contractions" should appear in Eps 16-20 range, and the Voice Evolution Tracker indicates "modern speech emerging" in Eps 16-20. By Ep 21, Ash should have begun using occasional contractions per the doc ("Late" range: medium contraction frequency). Consider adding 1-2 contractions in the rooftop scene, where his emotional state would logically erode the formal register. Something like "I can't" instead of "I cannot" in a moment of genuine anguish.

- THESSA: "We perverted it." — the reckoning applied to her own order. Three words that carry institutional guilt. Correct register: blunt, absolute, self-directed.
- ASH: "I was made to save gods. Not eat them." — the episode's thesis and the series' most important reframe. Eight words. The simplicity is Ash at his best: stripping away everything that isn't the point. Zero contractions — correct for the weight of the moment (formal register returns for statements about the divine).
- KAYA: The vessel revelation is his strongest exposition. "A spiritual shock absorber" — the academic finding the precise metaphor. "The order perverted it. Centuries ago." — Kaya delivering institutional judgment to Thessa is a scene that's been building since their first interaction. The formal register cracks only at "Yes" when Thessa asks if the Vow restores the original purpose — the single word carrying thirty years of vindication.
- MIKA: "So. The magic vow that saves Rune also saves Ash also saves the whole system. And the bad guy's plan is the opposite of all of that. Just making sure I have the summary." — peak Mika. **Pacing:** Four scenes. Safe house triage, Kindling revealed, Kaya's revelation, the ending. ## OVERALL ASSESSMENT

The 13-22 batch represents a significant escalation in both emotional and plot complexity, and the writing rises to meet it. The character voices are strong — Ash and Mika in particular are at their best. The kiss (Ep 18) is earned through careful escalation across Eps 14-17. The Vow reveal (Ep 19) handles the "mechanics, not existence" distinction cleanly. The god-eater-as-vessel revelation (Ep 22) is the series' most impactful reframe and lands with appropriate weight.

The AI-tell patterns flagged in the 1-12 QA have been largely addressed: "behind my sternum" is gone (overcorrected), the hum-closing formula is gone, the three-threat monologue appears only once. New patterns have emerged — "the particular," "the sound of someone who," and "a frequency that has no name" — that need attention before they calcify.

The primary structural concern is Ep 21's density. This is the one episode that cannot fit in ~5 minutes without losing content that matters. The secondary concern is the bond-hum closing dominance — 9 of 10 episodes end on bond-hum SFX, which creates a structural sameness that the varied descriptions mask but don't eliminate.

The gap between Rune's spoken and internal registers continues to be the show's engine, and it adapts well to the new demands of a Rune who is acting on his feelings rather than suppressing them. The two moments where the gap closes (Ep 14 bathroom silence, Ep 19 "I don't want to break the bond") are deliberate and earned.

Ready for production after addressing the critical and warning items above.
